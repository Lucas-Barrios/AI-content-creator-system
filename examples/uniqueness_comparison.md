## 4. Why Our Version Is Superior for SRH's Marketing Needs

### 4.1 Grounded claims build trust
ChatGPT writes what it can reasonably infer from SRH's public website. The result reads like content any competitor could produce about themselves — and often does. Our system injects details (block lengths, credit caps, programme names, alumni quotes) that only someone with access to internal SRH materials would know. That specificity is what separates credible thought-leadership from SEO filler.

### 4.2 Brand voice is non-negotiable for regulated audiences
Prospective students — especially international ones making a major life and financial decision — are highly sensitive to tone. ChatGPT defaults to American English ("emphasize", "strategize") and leans on superlatives ("strong choice", "clear advantage") that feel like advertising copy. Our system enforces British English, avoids superlatives, and centres the student's outcome rather than the institution's prestige. This is not a stylistic preference; it is a compliance requirement with SRH's brand guidelines.

### 4.3 Named people and programmes are conversion drivers
Marketing research consistently shows that named social proof (a real alumnus with a real job title) outperforms anonymous testimonials. ChatGPT cannot safely fabricate names without hallucinating. Our RAG system retrieves verified quotes from the knowledge base, giving the content legal and factual safety alongside persuasive power.

### 4.4 The gap widens with more niche content
This comparison used a broad, well-documented topic. For content like a specific dual-study programme, a new research initiative, or a faculty spotlight, ChatGPT's public-knowledge ceiling drops sharply. Our system's advantage scales with the specificity of the brief — which is exactly where marketing content needs to be most precise.
