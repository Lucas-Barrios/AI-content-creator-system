## 1. Brand Identity

### Who They Are
SRH University is a state-recognised private university headquartered in Berlin, Germany, with 18 campuses across the country. It operates across a wide range of disciplines — from business and law to design, health, and technology — and positions itself as a modern, internationally oriented institution with a strong focus on employability and practical learning.

### Brand Tagline
> **"Design your Future"**

This tagline anchors all top-level brand communication. It is action-oriented, personal, and forward-looking — placing agency firmly with the student.

### Core Brand Positioning
- **Practical over theoretical** — The CORE principle (Competence-Oriented Research and Education) is SRH's flagship differentiator: a skill and project-based learning model taught in 5-week focused blocks, recognised with the Genius Loci Award (2018).
- **Career-first** — Content is consistently framed around professional outcomes: roles, titles, salary trajectory, and network expansion.
- **International and inclusive** — The university markets strongly to non-German speakers; all MBA programmes are taught in English, and the site defaults to an English-language experience for international visitors.
- **Accreditation-led credibility** — Heavy use of third-party validation: system accreditation, CHE Ranking, AACSB membership, Wissenschaftsrat, family-friendly certification.

## 4. Voice and Tone

### Voice Characteristics
SRH's brand voice is **direct, aspirational, and practical**. It speaks to adults who are already professionally established and making a deliberate investment decision — not students fresh from school.

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| **Direct** | Sentences are short and declarative. No hedging. Claims are stated as fact. |
| **Aspirational** | Constantly oriented toward outcomes and the future self: CEO, CFO, Founder, General Manager. |
| **Warm but professional** | Not cold or corporate. Uses "you" and "your" throughout. Feels like a confident advisor, not a bureaucracy. |
| **Action-oriented** | Every section ends with a push: "Apply now", "Request information", "Learn more about CORE". |
| **Internationally minded** | English-first for MBA content. Avoids German-specific cultural references that would alienate international readers. |

### Tone by Context

| Context | Tone |
|---|---|
| Hero / headline copy | Bold, motivating, short. "Get prepared for your next career step." |
| Programme content | Informative but still outcome-framed. Features verbs like "strengthen", "deepen", "lead", "implement". |
| Admissions / process | Reassuring and step-by-step. Numbered lists. "We look forward to welcoming you." |
| Cost / financing | Transparent and matter-of-fact. Costs are stated clearly without softening language. |
| Faculty / testimonials | Credible and authoritative. Professor quotes add human gravitas. |
| CTAs | Urgent but not pushy. "Apply now", "Start your application now", "Request information". |

## 5. Messaging Pillars

These are the core themes that recur consistently across the EMBA page and should anchor all content:

### Pillar 1: Career Transformation
SRH does not sell education — it sells career elevation. Every piece of content connects programme content to professional outcomes. Role titles (CEO, CFO, Founder) are named explicitly to help the reader project themselves into the future.

> Example language: *"Become a CEO or Management Consultant"*, *"prepared for challenging management tasks"*

### Pillar 2: Practical Learning (The CORE Principle)
The CORE principle is SRH's most distinctive brand asset and should be referenced in any content discussing the learning experience. It signals that SRH is not a traditional lecture-hall university.

> Example language: *"skill and project-based academic approach"*, *"apply your knowledge directly to projects and your future career"*

### Pillar 3: Leadership and Soft Skills
A recurring emphasis on the human side of management — communication, team leadership, intercultural competence — differentiates SRH from purely technical business schools.

> Example language: *"strengthen your analytical, leadership and communication skills"*, *"lead diverse and international teams"*

### Pillar 4: Accreditation and Quality
SRH uses third-party proof points heavily. Accreditation bodies, rankings, and awards are displayed prominently as trust signals for an audience making a significant financial commitment.

> Example language: *"Quality is measurable"*, *"Study with excellence — our awards, accreditations and certifications"*

### Pillar 5: Berlin as an Asset
The Berlin location is not incidental — it is positioned as a feature. Being embedded in Germany's startup capital and international hub adds credibility to claims about networks, industry access, and career opportunities.

## 7. Imagery Style

Based on observed photography choices across the page:

- **People-first**: All imagery centres on people, not buildings or abstract concepts.
- **Diverse representation**: Multiple ethnicities and genders shown, reflecting international student body.
- **Modern office settings**: Backgrounds show collaborative workspaces, large windows, natural light — not traditional lecture halls.
- **Warm colour grading**: Photos lean warm (orange, amber tones) rather than cold or clinical.
- **Active, not posed**: Subjects are shown doing things — writing, holding tablets, collaborating — not standing formally for portraits.
- **One exception — red wall**: A striking image of a student walking past a bold red wall reinforces brand colour through environmental photography rather than UI design.

**Avoid:** Stock photo clichés (handshakes, pointing at whiteboards, generic laptop shots). SRH's imagery feels curated and slightly editorial.

## 8. CTAs and Conversion Language

SRH uses a two-CTA model consistently:

| Primary CTA | Secondary CTA |
|---|---|
| "Apply now" | "Request information" |

- The primary CTA is always application-focused and links directly to the apply portal.
- The secondary CTA captures leads who are not yet ready to apply — request forms are embedded in-page, not on a separate landing page.
- CTAs use direct imperative verbs. No softening language like "consider applying" or "find out if you qualify".

---

## 9. Banned Phrases and Patterns

Based on what is notably absent from SRH's copy:

- ❌ Passive voice constructions ("students are taught", "knowledge is imparted")
- ❌ Generic academic filler ("world-class education", "cutting-edge curriculum")
- ❌ Jargon without explanation ("synergistic learning ecosystem")
- ❌ Overly formal or distant register ("prospective candidates are encouraged to...")
- ❌ Vague outcome claims without role specifics ("great career prospects")

---

## 10. Content Do's and Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Use "you" and "your" throughout | Write in third person about students |
| Name specific job titles as outcomes | Use vague phrases like "career opportunities" |
| Reference the CORE principle when discussing learning | Describe teaching as lectures or theory-heavy |
| Lead with transformation, not admission requirements | Lead with bureaucratic process language |
| Use short, confident sentences | Use long, hedged academic prose |
| Anchor to Berlin as an advantage | Treat location as an afterthought |
| Show professor names and faces | Use anonymous institutional voice only |
